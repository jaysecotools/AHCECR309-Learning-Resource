Cultural Heritage in Tasmania

Understanding cultural heritage considerations is essential for conducting responsible site inspections in Tasmania.

What is Cultural Heritage?

Cultural heritage refers to places, objects, and traditions that communities have inherited from the past and want to preserve for future generations. In Tasmania, this includes both Aboriginal heritage and historic heritage from European settlement.

Tasmania's cultural heritage is diverse and includes:

  • Aboriginal sites and cultural landscapes
  • Historic buildings and structures
  • Industrial and archaeological sites
  • Cultural landscapes and modified natural areas
  • Movable cultural heritage objects

Key Principles for Heritage-Aware Inspections

When conducting site inspections with cultural heritage considerations:

Do:

  • Research the area's heritage potential before visiting
  • Look for both obvious and subtle heritage indicators
  • Document any potential heritage features with photos and notes
  • Consult with Aboriginal Heritage Tasmania if Aboriginal sites are suspected
  • Follow all legislative requirements for heritage protection

Don't:

  • Disturb or remove artifacts or materials from heritage sites
  • Assume that absence of visible features means no heritage values
  • Proceed with works if you suspect heritage values without proper assessment
  • Share sensitive information about Aboriginal heritage locations publicly

Types of Cultural Heritage Features

Cultural heritage can manifest in various forms. During inspections, look for these potential heritage indicators:

Aboriginal Cultural Heritage

  • Shell middens and stone artifacts
  • Rock art and engraving sites
  • Scarred trees (culturally modified trees)
  • Stone arrangements and ceremonial sites
  • Subsurface archaeological deposits

Historic Heritage

  • Historic buildings and structures
  • Archaeological sites (foundations, ruins)
  • Industrial sites (mines, kilns, factories)
  • Historic landscapes (gardens, pathways)
  • Artifacts and movable heritage

Aboriginal Heritage Recognition

Understanding and respecting Aboriginal heritage is a critical component of site inspections in Tasmania.

Tasmanian Aboriginal Heritage

Tasmanian Aboriginal heritage represents one of the oldest continuous cultures in the world, dating back over 40,000 years. Aboriginal people have maintained deep connections to Country despite the impacts of colonization.

Key aspects of Tasmanian Aboriginal heritage include:

  • Cultural landscapes reflecting deep spiritual connections to Country
  • Archaeological sites documenting thousands of years of occupation
  • Scarred trees marking cultural activities and resource use
  • Shell middens demonstrating coastal resource use
  • Rock art sites containing important cultural knowledge

Cultural Sensitivity Note:

Some Aboriginal heritage sites contain sensitive cultural information. It's important to follow appropriate protocols for documentation and information sharing, consulting with Aboriginal Heritage Tasmania and relevant Aboriginal communities.

Working with Aboriginal Communities

When Aboriginal heritage is identified or suspected during site inspections:

Recommended Engagement Process:

  1. Cease activity that may impact potential heritage values
  2. Contact Aboriginal Heritage Tasmania for guidance
  3. Follow established protocols for community consultation
  4. Respect cultural authority and decision-making processes
  5. Implement recommendations from Aboriginal heritage officers

Aboriginal communities are the primary knowledge holders for their heritage. Building respectful, collaborative relationships is essential for appropriate heritage management.

Legislative Requirements

Understanding the legal framework for cultural heritage protection in Tasmania is essential for compliance during site inspections.

Key Legislation

Several pieces of legislation protect cultural heritage in Tasmania:

Aboriginal Heritage Act 1975

The primary legislation protecting Aboriginal heritage in Tasmania. It:

  • Provides for the recognition and protection of Aboriginal sites
  • Establishes the Aboriginal Heritage Council
  • Requires permits for works that may affect Aboriginal heritage
  • Creates offenses for damaging Aboriginal heritage

Historic Cultural Heritage Act 1995

Protects historic heritage in Tasmania. It:

  • Establishes the Tasmanian Heritage Council
  • Provides for the Tasmanian Heritage Register
  • Requires works approval for registered places
  • Includes provisions for archaeological sites

Other Relevant Legislation

  • Land Use Planning and Approvals Act 1993: Requires consideration of heritage in planning schemes
  • Environmental Management and Pollution Control Act 1994: May require heritage considerations in environmental assessments
  • Commonwealth Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Heritage Protection Act 1984: Provides additional Commonwealth protection

Penalties for Non-Compliance:

Serious penalties apply for damaging cultural heritage, including:

  • Fines up to $1.5 million for corporations
  • Fines up to $300,000 for individuals
  • Imprisonment in serious cases
  • Restoration orders requiring remediation
